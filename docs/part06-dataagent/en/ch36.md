# Chapter 36 Data Analysis, Visualization, and Reporting

---
## Chapter Summary

This chapter discusses the presentation layer of DataAgent. After query and analysis produce results, business users ultimately need materials they can use for discussion, approval, and review: conclusions must be clear, charts should correspond to the questions, reports must allow evidence traceability, and recommendations need to indicate human verification boundaries. This chapter explains how DataAgent transitions from data results to business delivery through seven aspects: insight generation, chart selection, automated EDA, EvidenceRef, report structure, action recommendations, and output quality evaluation.
## Key Terms

Data insight, visualization, automated EDA, EvidenceRef, report generation, groundedness, HITL
## Learning Objectives

- Be able to distinguish between restating numbers, business insights, and action recommendations.
- Be able to select appropriate charts based on analytical intent and constrain chart data sources accordingly.
- Be able to design EvidenceRef so that report conclusions can trace back to SQL, Python, and metric versions.
- Be able to evaluate the output quality of DataAgent beyond just checking whether the SQL is correct.

---
## Opening Scenario

Chapters 34 and 35 have completed data retrieval and analysis: the system knows that East China’s operational GMV declined last week, knows the top SKUs, and also knows that when aggregated by category, a few categories contributed most of the decline. What the business users want next is not SQL, nor Python stdout, but a business report suitable for discussion.

A business report is different from a chat response. It must explain what happened, where the evidence comes from, which points are facts, which are inferences, and which recommendations require human verification. When the controller approves, they should be able to click on footnotes to see the original queries, Python outputs, metric versions, and data freshness. Otherwise, the smoother the report’s language, the greater the risk.

This chapter discusses exactly this level of expressive ability. It takes the results from Chapters 34 and 35 and organizes tables, statistics, charts, and evidence into a report. Chapter 30 defines the HITL state machine, Chapter 48 defines front-end rendering and interaction, Chapter 39 defines the evaluation pipeline; this chapter defines what the DataAgent’s output should look like.

## 36.1 From Results to Insights

Insights are not just translating numbers into natural language. Simply restating numbers, such as "GMV in East China Operations dropped by 12.3%," is not enough; insights go further by answering: where is the decline concentrated, which structural changes are related, and whether further action is warranted. A usable insight typically includes a comparison target, the magnitude of difference, concentration, evidence, and uncertainty.

*Table 36-1: Differences between Restatement, Insight, and Recommendation. Source: Compiled by the author.*

| Type       | Example                              | Purpose                          |
|------------|------------------------------------|---------------------------------|
| Restatement| East China Operations GMV dropped 12.3% | Tells the user what happened    |
| Insight    | SKUs A/B/C account for 58% of the decline | Shows where the problem is concentrated |
| Inference  | The increase in the daily chemical category’s share may be related to promotion end | Proposes directions to verify    |
| Recommendation | Category manager to evaluate SKU-A’s promotion and restocking strategy | Moves into business action and approval |

When Planner generates insights, it should not have the model recalculate contributions. Contributions, percentages, year-over-year and month-over-month data should be pre-calculated via SQL or Python. The model’s job is to organize language, prioritize, and link evidence. If the Python output does not give “58%,” that figure should not appear in the report.

Insights must also distinguish between fact, inference, and hypothesis. Facts come directly from aggregations or scripts; inferences are evidence-based but still require business confirmation; hypotheses are mere directions to test. Mixing these three confuses the report’s tone, making it seem decisive while actually complicating approval.

The quantity of insights must be controlled. A single management report should not list every anomaly, but should select 3 to 5 key conclusions focused around the core issue. In the East China decline case, the core is regional GMV decrease, so Top SKUs, category contributions, data quality, and causes to verify suffice; adding stores, channels, members, and weather turns the report into a collection of observations. DataAgent should help the user converge on the key insights rather than showcase every pattern it can find.

Insight text should avoid absolute statements. Without promotional calendar evidence, one cannot write “Decline caused by end of promotion,” but only “Timing of decline may relate to promotion end, needs verification with promotional calendar.” This cautious wording clarifies for the Controller which conclusions may be directly cited and which require follow-up confirmation.

## 36.2 Chart Selection

Charts serve the purpose of comparison. Use line charts for trends, horizontal bar charts for rankings and contributions, stacked bars or area charts for composition, histograms or box plots for distributions, and scatter plots for correlations. DataAgent should not choose 3D bar charts, overly colorful palettes, or pie charts with a dozen slices just because the model finds them "prettier."

In the East China case, Top SKU contribution is better represented by horizontal bar charts. Bar charts clearly show which SKUs have the largest delta and easily link to row-level EvidenceRef. To show category share changes, use stacked bars or grouped bars. To observe multi-week trends, use line charts.

Chart specs are controlled artifacts, not freeform image prompts. The `x_field`, `y_field`, sorting, aggregation, and color fields must all exist in the input schema or Python artifact. The `chart_renderer` must validate field existence, data reference consistency, and metric version visibility, then return `chart_id`, `data_ref`, and evidence hash. Chapter 48 covers frontend rendering and interaction—models cannot directly produce untraceable images.

Automated visualization tools like LIDA follow the process: "summarize data, enumerate targets, generate charts, evaluate results." DataAgent can borrow this multi-stage approach but should be more conservative in enterprise scenarios: determine chart type by rules first, then let the model fill in specs, and finally validate with schema and EvidenceRef.

Charts must also fit report reading contexts. Business meeting materials typically require quick comparisons, so titles, axes, units, and scopes must be immediately visible. Interactive exploration pages can allow filtering and drill-down, but exported PDFs or screenshots must keep sufficient context. Chart titles specifying “Operational GMV `gmv_ops@2025Q1`” are preferable over just “GMV Delta Contribution” for auditability and sharing.

Colors and sorting must also be controlled. Positive and negative deltas should use consistent semantic colors. Ranking charts must clearly state whether sorting is by absolute delta or contribution percentage, and whether items outside Top-N are merged into “Others.” If the model arbitrarily selects colors and sorting, the same report type will become hard to compare week to week.

## 36.3 Automated EDA and Anomaly Detection

Automated EDA is not simply about delivering profiling reports directly to users; rather, it automatically generates a small set of structured signals in diagnostic tasks, such as missing rates, outliers, distribution shifts, changes in top categories, and sample size risks. For example, in the East China sales decline scenario, if the SKU missing rate is slightly elevated, the report should flag that the Top SKU ranking might be affected.

The methods used in automated EDA must be clearly documented. For instance, “SKU-A is 2.4 standard deviations below the mean of the previous week” is more credible than simply stating “AI detected an anomaly.” Statistical methods, thresholds, input data, and hashes should be recorded in artifacts to serve as references in the report. Anomaly alerts without methodological explanations easily become another form of model hallucination.

The level of EDA should be controlled. Basic data queries can disable automated EDA; diagnostic tasks can run lightweight automated EDA; subscription-based task workbenches are appropriate for proactive anomaly detection. Excessive EDA slows down simple investigations and fills reports with marginal signals, which can negatively impact business decision-making.

Discoveries from automated EDA should not be automatically upgraded to insights. Missing rates, outliers, and distribution shifts are only candidate signals—Planners must judge their relevance to the user’s problem. In the East China decline case, a slight increase in SKU missing rates affecting the Top SKU ranking warrants inclusion in quality alerts; anomalies in unrelated fields should not be included in the executive summary. Otherwise, the report risks confusing data quality noise with true business findings.

When EDA detects severe quality issues, the system should downgrade the delivery quality. Minor quality warnings can be added as footnotes in the report; severe quality failures should cause rejection of the formal report generation or trigger a Human-In-The-Loop (HITL) review. The presentation layer must not “polish over” upstream quality risks, as business users typically only see the final materials.
## 36.4 EvidenceRef and Report Structure

EvidenceRef is the core contract of a DataAgent report. It binds a specific insight to the data source that produced it: which Tool, which artifact, which row or aggregation, which Metric version, and which content hash. Reports can rephrase wording but must not drop the evidence reference.

```json
{
  "ref_id": "ev_002",
  "source_tool": "python_sandbox@v1",
  "artifact_id": "df_sku_contrib",
  "locator": "row:sku_id=SKU-A",
  "metric_id": "gmv_ops",
  "metric_version": "2025Q1",
  "content_hash": "sha256:8f3a2b1c"
}
```

When users click a footnote, the frontend should locate the corresponding row or aggregate result based on `artifact_id` and `locator`, verify the content hash, then display the Metric version, data timestamp, and source Tool. A free-text "Source: Python analysis result" does not provide audit value.

The report structure can remain stable. Business analysis reports typically include an executive summary, questions and scope, data overview, findings, recommendations, and appendix. The executive summary is limited to 3 to 5 points, each with an EvidenceRef; questions and scope show the Question Frame, metric version, and time range; the appendix retains Run ID, SQL hash, Python hash, freshness, and a full evidence list.

Business meetings, regulatory submissions, and external materials should enter HITL (Human In The Loop). DataAgent can generate draft reports, but Controllers, Legal, or business owners must confirm before publication. The value of HITL is not just "click to approve," but also recording rejection reasons, annotations, and revision history into the Trace, forming samples for future evaluation.

Report editing must protect the evidence chain. Users may change titles, rephrase, delete paragraphs, or request new charts, but an insight’s original conclusion must not lose its EvidenceRef. If the edited sentence is no longer supported by original evidence, the system should require re-binding evidence or mark the sentence as manually supplemented. This allows human editing without breaking traceability.

EvidenceRef must also support version fixing. After report publication, upstream artifacts may be cleaned or recalculated and semantic layer Metrics may be upgraded. Published reports should continue referencing the artifact hash and Metric version at the time of publication—not automatically tracking the latest data. If users choose to refresh the report, a new Run and new EvidenceRef should be generated.

The appendix is not an optional engineering dump. Formal reports must at least keep Run ID, metric version, SQL hash, Python hash, chart data_ref, freshness, and quality status. Business users may not open the appendix regularly, but when disputes arise, the appendix determines whether a report can be reviewed and audited.
## 36.5 Actionable Recommendations and Business Feedback Loop

An agent should not make business decisions on behalf of the business. Recommendations in reports should be framed as "who needs to evaluate what," rather than directly issuing business actions. Actions like restocking, price adjustments, promotions, and channel changes involve inventory, gross margin, supply chain, and organizational responsibility. The DataAgent can only propose options based on evidence.

Recommendations should be tiered. Observations state facts, inferences explain possible reasons, recommendations suggest actionable steps and indicate approval status. For example, in the East China case, "SKU-A contribution dropped 32%" is a fact; "possibly related to promotion ending" is an inference; "category manager to evaluate SKU-A promotion and restocking strategy" is a recommendation.

The business feedback loop comes from response. User acceptance, rejection, delay, or modification of recommendations should all be recorded in the Eval and Playbook candidate pools. High acceptance of recommendations can form the basis of standard analysis procedures; high rejection rates indicate model over-inference, insufficient evidence, or an unsuitable report template for the current business.

Recommendations must also have a responsible owner. Writing "recommend optimizing promotion strategy" is too vague; writing "recommend category manager check SKU-A promotion end date and restocking changes" is more actionable. The responsible party can come from the organizational context or manual selection but must never be arbitrarily made up by the model. If the platform cannot identify the responsible party, it should say "requires business owner assignment" rather than inventing a department.

The results of the feedback loop can be used to iteratively improve report templates. If a certain type of recommendation is repeatedly rejected, it may indicate it exceeds the DataAgent’s evidence scope; if a type of insight is repeatedly accepted, it can be formalized as a Playbook and fixed chart. The DataAgent’s reporting capability is not about generating text once but about continuously calibrating through feedback what expressions are truly useful to the business.

## 36.6 Output Quality Evaluation

DataAgent cannot rely solely on exact match of SQL queries. The most common user complaints are usually about charts being off-topic, conclusions lacking evidence, failure to ask follow-up questions when needed, and unclear report definitions. Output evaluation should cover answers, insights, charts, reports, refusals, and follow-up questioning.

*Table 36-2: Dimensions of DataAgent Output Evaluation. Source: Compiled in this book.*

| Dimension | Checkpoints |
|---|---|
| Answer | Numerical tolerance, ranking consistency, definition footnotes |
| Insight | Whether each insight has a valid EvidenceRef |
| Chart | Whether chart type matches intent, fields come from schema |
| Report | Run ID, Metric version, freshness, appendix completeness |
| Interaction | Whether ambiguous definitions trigger follow-up questions, whether refusal occurs when evidence is insufficient |

Groundedness is the core metric. Rule validation first checks existence of `locator`, `content_hash`, Metric version, and artifact; LLM-as-Judge can assist to judge whether the narrative aligns with evidence, but cannot replace the hard validation of hash and locator. Every insight in the report should withstand clicking and replay verification.

Negative samples are important. The evaluation set should include bad cases such as insights without EvidenceRef, charts with nonexistent fields, models internally calculating contribution, inferences presented as facts, missing definition versions, etc. Without these negative samples, the system may perform well in demos but run out of control in real reports.

Online signals should also be incorporated into evaluation. Manual report revision rates, approval rejection rates, chart regeneration rates, user negative feedback, and suggestion adoption rates all reflect the quality of the expression layer. If SQL is correct but reports are frequently rejected, the issue lies not in data retrieval but in insight organization, definition explanation, or recommendation boundaries.

Evaluation should also cover edited reports. Many systems only assess first drafts but ignore whether evidence remains valid after manual modifications. The report editor should revalidate EvidenceRef upon saving: whether the references exist, hashes match, sentences remain evidence-supported, and chart fields still correspond to the data. The edited product is the final deliverable—it is insufficient to only ensure the first draft passes.

LLM-as-Judge can assess language clarity and insight relevance but cannot replace hard checks. Numerical values, sorting, evidence location, Metric version, and data freshness must all be checked by rules or programs. Model review is suited to help detect “does this paragraph read like a business report,” but not to decide “does this 58% actually come from the data.”

## 36.7 East China Decline Report — Sample

The following is the report skeleton for the East China decline Run. The sample data is included solely to illustrate structure; currency units and decimal precision should be handled uniformly by the report template.

**Title**: East China Region Last-Week Operational GMV Decline Post-Mortem (Pending Controller Approval)

**Run ID**: `run-8f3a`

**Metric**: Operational GMV `gmv_ops@2025Q1`

**Data as of**: 2025-06-14 06:00

**Executive Summary**

- East China's operational GMV last week fell 12.3% week-over-week; the regional gap was approximately −¥13.1 million. [`ev_001`]
- SKU-A, SKU-B, and SKU-C together account for 58% of the decline gap; SKU-A alone contributes 32%. [`ev_002`]
- The share of the personal-care category rose by 4 percentage points, consistent with the categories of the top declining SKUs. This point is an inference and requires confirmation from the category manager. [`ev_004`]
- The null rate for `sku_id` is slightly elevated; the top-SKU ranking should be treated as indicative only. [`ev_005`]

**Findings**

Fact: SKU-A gap is −¥4.2 million, accounting for 32% of the regional decline.

Inference: The rising share of the personal-care category may be related to the end of a promotion or a structural mix shift.

Hypothesis: Whether cross-regional channel diversion exists has not been determined — channel-level detail was not incorporated into this Run, so no conclusion is drawn at this time.

**Recommendations**

The category manager is advised to conduct a promotional post-mortem for SKU-A, reconciling the promotion end date against replenishment and planogram changes. This recommendation requires Controller approval before it enters the downstream task queue.

**Appendix**

The appendix should contain the Semantic SQL hash, Python script hash, metric version, data freshness, quality alerts, the EvidenceRef list, and the report approval record. Readers need not open the appendix on every review, but it must be accessible for audits and post-mortems.

If the Controller rejects the report, the system must retain the rejection rationale. For example, a note such as "the personal-care category mix shift lacks promotional-calendar evidence" can serve as a data-backfill task for the next Run, or it can be added to the evaluation set to prevent the model from presenting the same type of inference as established fact in the future. The report is not an endpoint — it is the interface through which data tasks enter the organization's operational workflow.

If the report is approved, the publication event must also enter the Trace. The online page, PDF, exported files, and notification messages should all be bound to the same `report_id` and `run_id`. When someone later forwards the PDF, the Run ID in the footer carries the static file back to the platform's evidence chain.

## 36.8 Mini-Platform Implementation Path

The presentation layer can be divided into two Tools and one template directory: `chart_renderer` is responsible for chart spec generation and validation, `report_renderer` or the DataAgent template is responsible for the report artifacts, and `core/eval/` handles output evaluation. Both charts and reports only read upstream artifacts and do not recompute core numbers.

```text
mini-platform/tools/chart_renderer/
├── handler.py
└── spec_schema.json

mini-platform/agents/data_agent/templates/
└── weekly_ops.md
```

Before going live, at least four things must be checked. First, the chart `data_ref` must have the same source as the SQL/Python artifacts. Second, every insight must have an EvidenceRef. Third, external reports are by default subject to HITL (Human In The Loop). Fourth, the report footer must include the Run ID and `metric_id@version`. These requirements may seem trivial, but lacking any one of them makes report replay and traceability very difficult.

Templates must also limit information density. The executive summary should not exceed 5 points, charts should not overcrowd a page, and inferences and assumptions must be clearly labeled. The goal of DataAgent reports is to assist business meetings and decision-making—not to showcase how much text the system can generate.

In implementation, `chart_renderer`, report templates, and Eval should not each maintain their own evidence fields. The EvidenceRef schema should be centrally defined and reused across Tool Results, report artifacts, front-end footnotes, and evaluators. Once fields diverge, it often leads to situations where charts can be traced but reports cannot, or the evidence referenced by reports cannot be opened in the front end.

The first release can support just one report template and two or three chart types. A narrower scope with well-built evidence chains and HITL is more important than simultaneously supporting PPT, PDF, web, and multiple chart types. Complexity in the presentation layer comes from evidence and editing, not from the number of file formats.

After report publication, withdrawal must be supported. Data quality rollbacks, metric definition revisions, approval changes, or permission adjustments may require retracting published materials. Withdrawal should not delete historical evidence but mark report status, retain withdrawal reasons, and prevent old links from continuing to spread as valid conclusions. This both protects the audit trail and avoids incorrect reports being cited by the business going forward.
If the report has been distributed to multiple recipients, the withdrawal event should also notify the original recipients and display the latest status on the report page.
Status synchronization must be auditable.

## Chapter Recap

1. The expression layer organizes SQL and Python results into business materials that are discussable, approvable, and traceable.
2. Insights must address concentration, structure, and actionable significance; they cannot simply restate numbers.
3. Chart specifications must be linked with input data and evidence hashes; generating untraceable images is not allowed.
4. EvidenceRef is the core contract ensuring report credibility—each key insight should be clickable back to its evidence.
5. Output evaluation must cover insights, charts, reports, refusals, and follow-up questions; focusing solely on SQL accuracy is insufficient.
